North Middletown, Kentucky • Est. 1948 • Home of the Kings

Save North Middletown Elementary School.

The district's best elementary school is the one it has marked to close. On July 15, 2026, a planning committee voted to reclassify North Middletown Elementary as transitional, the first step toward closing it. I went through the district's own audits, state data, and two decades of public records. Here is what the numbers say. Every one of them is checkable, and I want you to check them.

Part One

The money problem is real. Closing NMES does not solve it.

The district ran a General Fund deficit of $2.65 million last year before transfers, after $2.54 million the year before; two years running is what makes it structural. Reserves are falling by about $1.1 million a year. I do not dispute any of that. The question is whether closing a 128-student school fixes it. It does not come close. The most recent year also improved: the district's own unaudited FY2026 close, presented to the board on July 16, 2026, shows the year ending down about $374,000, with revenues $2.2 million over budget and salaries under budget.

What actually drove the deficit

Federal pandemic aid wound down, taking about $2.95 million a year with it, and attendance-based state funding fell with roughly 248 fewer funded students in average daily attendance once the pandemic hold-harmless ended. Meanwhile, costs the district controls grew fast: central-office administration rose 44.8 percent in two years.

A fair caution I print beside this number: Kentucky audits fold state pension allocations into these expense lines, so some share may be accounting rather than hiring. That is why the report asks for a position-by-position accounting instead of assuming the worst.

Statement of Activities, both audits

The tax picture the budget talk leaves out

Bourbon County Schools taxes real estate at 52.4 cents per $100, second lowest of nine area districts and roughly 13 cents below the statewide school average. The board also holds a no-recall option to raise revenue 4 percent a year. The math is simple and it does not bend: either spending comes down or revenue goes up. Standing idle is the one answer I rule out.

The bonds: a different pot of money

Kentucky law walls building money off from operating money; none of it can pay teachers. The $6.055 million bond issued in 2024 flowed into the construction fund, where construction in progress grew from $3.65 million to $7.41 million in one year, and it is why the district's debt payments jump this year. Its purpose is on the public record: the state bond disclosure, approved by the legislature's Capital Projects and Bond Oversight Committee in June 2024, lists a high school roof replacement and a districtwide audio system. No bond on record names NMES. And closing a school creates no borrowing room: bonding capacity is built from restricted streams, a $100 per student state allotment and the nickel building tax, that do not grow when a school closes. The FY2024 audit already showed about $23.5 million in unused capacity as of June 30, 2024. Section 6 of the report walks through the statute and breaks out every component.

The $14 million plan, and the levers not on the table

At the July 15 planning meeting, the superintendent said the district must free up $800,000 to $1,000,000 a year in operating money to bond $14 million. That is a general fund bond: the payment would come from the budget that pays teachers, and at roughly $0.8 to $1.1 million a year it is about the same size as the school's claimed operating cost. Under this plan, money saved by a closure would go to debt service, so the General Fund deficit itself would not shrink.

The claimed savings cannot carry that bond anyway. Bond capacity is yearly savings multiplied over the life of the bonds. The district's own school-level spending reports, filed with the state every year since 2019, put NMES's full excess cost near $121,000 a year, which supports about $1.6 million of bonds, not $14 million. Even my most generous case, $725,000, supports about $9.4 million. Only the administration's own unpublished $900,000 figure reaches the target.

Three funding sources are available that do not involve a closure. The audit's $23.5 million in bonding potential, less the 2024 issue, leaves roughly $18 million of restricted capacity, and the district's own debt schedule shows payments falling from $1.58 million to about $400,000 a year as old bonds retire after 2030, room a standard wrap-around bond structure is designed to use. A recallable nickel would raise about $920,000 a year on our $1.84 billion assessment, plus state matching dollars, supporting $12 million or more; it is the funding mechanism Kentucky law provides for facility construction. SFCC state offers add more. These sources raise amounts comparable to the plan's $14 million, so the decision is a choice among funding mechanisms, and it should be made with the fiscal agent's current bonding potential statement public. The model's Debt_Service tab now runs a combined scenario, balancing the operating budget first from levy increments and cost reductions, then funding construction from the remainder plus the nickel: roughly $30 million of total capacity in the conservative case and about $50 million on the FY2026 trend, with the budget balanced first either way.

One more thing the board's own packet shows: the unaudited FY2026 close. The district's KDE Budget Monitoring Tool, in the July 16, 2026 board agenda, shows the year ending with revenues $2.2 million over budget, salaries $588,000 under budget and $224,000 below last year, and the General Fund down about $374,000, after audited drawdowns of $1.07 million and $1.23 million the two years before. One caveat, and I flag it myself: that includes a $1.57 million miscellaneous receipt budgeted at zero, and the district should identify it. If it is one-time money, the underlying gap is nearer $1.9 million. Either way, this is the third straight year actual results came in more than $2.5 million better than budget. The financial case presented to the planning committee so far cites budgeted figures; these are the actuals.

On the state's official 2024-25 tests, North Middletown is first among all four elementary schools in the county, in every tested subject, and beats the statewide elementary average in science and writing. Two decades of scores: the state's record and the third-party index

KDE official composite = Kentucky's overall accountability score in each era (2012 to 2016 and 2022 to 2025; none was issued from 2017 to 2021, and the 2006 to 2011 CATS files are available from KDE by data request). KDE reading and math average = percent proficient or better on the state tests, 2012 to 2019 and 2021 to 2025; 2020 tests were canceled and 2021 was a limited COVID administration. The SchoolDigger index is a third-party 0-100 rank of the same tests, kept for context: validated against the official record, it tracks the larger schools closely but is unreliable year to year for a school of NMES's size, and where the two disagree the official record governs.

Room to grow: enrollment since 1989

The building held 261 children at its peak. It holds 128 today against a rated capacity of 174, a rating the district's own plans put at 198 as recently as 2013. The seats are there. The answer is to fill them and grow NMES into the strongest elementary school in the region. A specialized program for advanced learners, the magnet model Kentucky's larger districts have run for decades, is one natural path; a preschool satellite or simply growing the neighborhood school are others, and every child in the zone keeps their seat under all of them. Each cell reads enrolled/rated capacity. The Today column compares current enrollment with current ratings: 174 at North Middletown, 549 at Bourbon Central (reflecting the addition in its 2021 plan), and 422 at Cane Ridge, which is 31 students over.

Then the 2026 draft, presented July 15 before the committee's amendment, made the paper-capacity point for us. With no new construction anywhere in it, the draft re-rates Cane Ridge from 422 up to 547 and Bourbon Central to 640, while cutting NMES from 174 to 154. Ratings that move 125 seats in one planning cycle are policy, not walls. Even at the draft's own 154, NMES is 83 percent full today, and the draft as presented still listed the school as permanent.

The architect's assessment behind the planning cycle, the KFICS presentation by RossTarrant Architects, is archived here too, and it makes the building case for keeping the school. NMES's total need is $8.5 million, the second lowest of the district's schools, against $8.8 million at Bourbon Central, $14.4 million at Cane Ridge (a 1992 building), $22.4 million at the middle school, $27.5 million at the high school, and $98.4 million districtwide. Closing the second-least-needy building solves none of that. And the same slides print yet another set of receiving-school capacities, 499 and 397, at which those schools have negative net room at today's enrollment. Run the seat math under every set the district has printed: 59 uncommitted at 549 and 422, 31 at the 2021 plan's printed 521, minus 16 at the architect's 499 and 397. Only the draft table's unexplained 640 and 547 produces room, and that is the set that added 216 paper seats with no construction.

Net uncommitted seats at the two receiving schools: 59 • Children who would need them: 128

Three things fall out of the record. NMES stood 93 percent full against its rating as recently as the 2021 plan; the "half-empty school" is four years old, not a generation old. Its rated capacity was written down from 198 to 174 between plans, a paper number the district itself controls through room assignments; and if ratings can be edited to make room in Paris, the same pencil can raise NMES back toward the 198 it held in 2013 and let it bring in even more students. And NMES's major renovation, priced at $1.9 million in the 2013 plan, reappears in the 2021 plan within a $4.3 million total need, the renovation scheduled behind other work both times, while both plans classified the school as permanent. The 2026 draft's "transitional" label reverses two consecutive state-approved plans.

Part Four

There is more money in fixing this than in closing the school.

Every figure below is a conservative estimate, each one sourced, and you can change the assumptions yourself in the downloadable model. Even the low end beats the most generous closure estimate, and none of it takes a school from a town.

The map the rebalancing plan stands on

Half the county's people live in Paris, where both receiving schools sit. The official federal boundary file (School Attendance Boundary Survey, 2015-16) puts the NMES zone at 110 square miles, 38 percent of the county, at roughly 1.2 students per square mile, the exact variable state law funds busing on. Cane Ridge's zone serves the north, Bourbon Central's the southwest. Closing the one eastern school keeps every square mile of that territory and serves it with longer rides, an estimated four road miles more each way on average and up to ten from town; rebalancing shortens them. Boston showed how much routing alone can do: an MIT algorithm made its bus routes 20 percent more efficient and saved $5 million in one year.

Part Five

Ten questions the board should answer in writing before any vote.

These are not rhetorical. Each has a document behind it that the administration either already holds or should be required to produce. Tap to read.

What is the real net saving from closure, published as a worksheet?

Line by line: costs that truly disappear, minus added transportation, receiving-school costs, and the carrying or disposal cost of the building.

What is inside the "over a million dollars" figure?

How does it reconcile with the state's published per-student spending data for the school? And which enrollment count is being used? Federal data show 128 students, while public statements have ranged lower.

Grade by grade, what is the real capacity at Bourbon Central and Cane Ridge?

The district's own facility plans rate them at 549 and 422; at today's 459 and 453 enrolled, that is a net 59 uncommitted seats for 128 children, with Cane Ridge already 31 over. And reconcile the ratings: the approved plans and the 2026 draft print three different capacities for the same unchanged buildings. What staff, sections, or space must be added, at what cost, from which fund?

How long will the youngest children ride the bus?

Publish the modeled routes and the longest one-way ride a North Middletown kindergartner would face.

Where is the full report behind the KFICS assessment slides?

The architect's presentation attaches $8.5 million to NMES, second lowest of the district's schools, against $23.2 million at the receiving schools and $98.4 million districtwide. Publish the complete assessment, its room-by-room data, and its assumptions.

Which of the claimed savings can actually pay teachers?

Which are General Fund dollars, and which are restricted facility dollars that cannot pay a single salary?

Where did the 2024 $6.055 million bond actually go, building by building?

The state bond disclosure already names the purpose (a high school roof and a districtwide audio system). Publish the BG-1 project applications behind it, and state when NMES last received meaningful capital investment.

Why did central-office administration grow 44.8 percent in two years while attendance fell?

What rollback is on the table before a school closes? Publish administrator compensation from the official state records.

What is the written plan to protect the students academically?

For children moved from the school that leads the county in every tested subject on the state's 2024-25 assessments into the two schools it outscores, and what happens to the district's Title I allocations when they move?

Which alternatives has the administration actually modeled, and with what results?

If none, why is closure first on the list rather than last?

The open records checklist

Every estimate in this analysis is built to be replaced by a district document. Kentucky's Open Records Act (KRS 61.870 to 61.884; request procedures in KRS 61.872) entitles any resident to these on request to the district's records custodian, with a response due in five business days. Fifteen requests, four groups:

The money. The net-savings worksheet behind the "over a million dollars" statement. Any alternatives modeling the administration has performed. Administrator compensation, position by position, five years. The levy rate-type history and the General Fund vs building fund cent split.

The buildings and bonds. The 2024 bond BG-1 project applications (its stated purpose is already public: a high school roof and a districtwide audio system), and the 2023 bond's purpose. KDE's bonding potential statement. The complete KFICS assessment behind the July slides, with room-by-room data. The room-by-room worksheet behind the capacity ratings, and the pre-2021 facility plans.

The boundaries and buses. The district's GIS attendance-zone map. Geocoded student counts by attendance area. The T-1 transportation report, route sheets, and cost per bus-mile. Modeled post-closure routes and the longest one-way ride for the youngest riders.

The students. The academic transition plan and Title I reallocation analysis. Grade-by-grade capacity and space at Bourbon Central and Cane Ridge. School-level climate and safety survey results for all three elementaries.

Nothing here seeks student-identifiable information. The full table, with what each item settles, is Appendix B of the report. Some checks need no request at all: the federal School Attendance Boundary Survey published the district's attendance-zone boundaries as free GIS files, and NCES publishes geocoded school locations.

Road Ahead

How a closure would actually happen, and where you come in.

A school is not closed by a single vote. Under Kentucky's facility-planning rules, it takes a chain of public meetings and separate votes by the planning committee, the elected school board, and the Kentucky Board of Education. Most of those steps are still ahead of us. Each one below lists the procedural rules the district must follow, because every rule is also a chance for the public to be heard, and a step that skips a rule can be challenged.

  1. DoneSchool board • January 2026

    The board appointed the Local Planning Committee

    A committee tasked with drafting the four-year facility plan.

    Rules that had to be met
    • 10 to 20 members, including parents, teachers, principals, the facilities director, central office staff, community leaders, and one board member
    • The superintendent serves but does not vote
    • Approved at an advertised public board meeting
  2. DonePlanning committee • May 27, 2026

    Orientation and the first public meeting

    Rules that had to be met
    • Advertised at least 7 days ahead in the largest-circulation local paper
    • State-led orientation; the committee selects a chair and vice-chair
  3. DonePlanning committee • July 15, 2026

    The committee recommended the "transitional" label

    A recommendation only. It does not close the school; it lets a draft plan be written without it.

    Rules that had to be met
    • Public forum advertised at least 7 days ahead, held after 5:30 p.m. on weekdays, in a wheelchair-accessible place
    • Open to public comment; subject to the Open Meetings Act
  4. Still aheadPlanning committee

    More public forums, then the committee's vote on the draft

    The committee reviews the state's comments and votes whether to accept the draft plan.

    Rules that must be met
    • At least 3 public forums and 3 committee meetings in all; forums advertised 7 days ahead, later meetings posted online 24 hours ahead with the paper notified
    • Held after 5:30 p.m. on weekdays, wheelchair-accessible, under the Open Meetings Act
    • The draft goes to the state for review before the last meeting
    • The accept vote requires a quorum of the committee
  5. Still aheadSchool board

    The board's first vote: whether to hold a public hearing

    Rules that must be met
    • Taken at a scheduled, advertised board meeting
    • If the board accepts the draft, it must schedule a public hearing and appoint a neutral hearing officer
  6. Still aheadPublic hearing

    The public hearing. This is your moment.

    Anyone may speak for or against the plan, or hand in a written statement to be read into the record.

    Rules that must be met
    • Advertised at least 14 days ahead in the largest-circulation paper, held after 5:30 p.m. on weekdays, wheelchair-accessible
    • A recording device and a public-address system are required
    • Copies of the draft placed for public review in the community beforehand
    • The hearing officer records every comment and stays neutral
    • Written statements are accepted with a name and address; anonymous ones are not read
  7. Still aheadSchool board

    The board's second vote: whether to adopt the final plan

    Rules that must be met
    • The hearing officer's report comes back to the board at a regular or special advertised meeting
    • Final adoption is by simple majority
  8. Still aheadKentucky Board of Education

    The state board must approve it

    Only after this does anything take effect, and only for four years.

    Rules that must be met
    • The full package is submitted to the state 45 days before the vote, with proof of every advertisement, the committee and board minutes, the signed hearing report, and the hearing recording
    • The state board meets only in February, April, June, August, October, and December
    • A vote of the Kentucky Board of Education is required to approve the plan

What makes a school "transitional"

Separate from the steps above, Kentucky keeps a school as a permanent center when it meets four of six criteria. Meet fewer than four, and it can be labeled transitional. Here are the six, and where North Middletown stands today.

  • 1. Test scores. Meets the state's curriculum threshold in one of the last two testing cycles. NMES leads the county, it meets this.
  • 2. Bus rides. Under 45 minutes for most young students. As a neighborhood school, NMES meets this today. Closure is what breaks it.
  • 3. Cost per student. Within 150 percent of the district's lowest-cost like school. NMES is about 107 percent of the lowest, it meets this.
  • 4. Equal programs. Offers 90 percent of what similar schools offer.
  • 5. Building condition. At least 30 years of life left, with renovation under 80 percent of replacement cost.
  • 6. Community support. Volunteer hours, PTA/PTO membership, an outside sponsor, and a community service program.
Closing North Middletown would be the board's choice, not a rule it has to follow. Even when a school meets these criteria, Kentucky lets the board apply the transitional label anyway, through a separate, discretionary path. Meeting the state's own standard for a permanent school is not an automatic shield. So the fair question to put to the district, in writing, is simple: why label this school transitional when it meets that standard? Ask for their written scoring on all six criteria.
The next public meetings are where this is decided. Show up, speak, and send a signed statement if you cannot attend.
