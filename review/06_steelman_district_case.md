# Opposing Counsel Review — "Saving North Middletown Elementary" (v2.2) and savenmes.org

Scope: full 30-page PDF (`Saving_North_Middletown_Elementary.pdf`) and `index.html`. Page cites are PDF page numbers; the printed footer ("Page N") runs one behind (PDF p.6 = printed p.5).

---

## 1. THE DISTRICT'S STRONGEST CASE — arguments the report fails to neutralize or handles weakly

### 1.1 The staffing-absorption attack: "eight to nine teachers do NOT all move"
- **Where the report opens the door:** PDF p.6 — "The 128 children would still need teachers, about eight to nine classrooms' worth at the district's average ratios." PDF p.17 concedes NMES runs "nine classroom sections… an average of about 14 students." Statutory caps are 24 (primary).
- **Why exploitable:** The report's entire savings ceiling ($640K, p.6: "the math tops out around $640,000") depends on assuming teachers transfer 1:1 with students. The district's rebuttal writes itself: 128 kids split across two schools is ~10-11 added children per grade per school; many land in existing sections under cap. If the district eliminates 7-9 of the 9 sections rather than 3-5, gross savings run 9 × $85K ≈ $765K + the report's own $290K fixed layer ≈ **$1.05M — which retroactively validates the superintendent's "over a million dollars"** using the report's own coefficients. The website calculator (index.html, slider `sPos` capped at `max="6"`, formula `net=290000+p*85000-b-l*4626`) literally forbids the district's scenario — a rigged-slider accusation waiting to happen.
- **SEVERITY: CRITICAL.** This is the load-bearing number of the whole campaign ($250K-$600K appears on the hero strip, the calculator, the plans table, the board threshold).
- **FIX:** Model the full-absorption case explicitly and honestly (raise the slider ceiling to 9; show that even then, net savings after busing, attrition of families, and receiving-school adds stay under ~$900K and under 35% of the deficit — because the capacity constraint forces added sections). Publish the grade-by-grade absorption table you're demanding from the district, using their published enrollments, before they publish theirs.

### 1.2 The capacity argument is self-detonating
- **Where:** PDF p.12-13: "Rated capacity is a policy output the district shapes through its own room assignments… the same pencil can raise North Middletown's rating back toward the 198." Versus PDF p.13 and the site's totalstrip: "a net 59 uncommitted seats for 128 children. The seats are not there."
- **Why exploitable:** You cannot argue rated capacity is a soft, editable "pencil" number when it inflates NMES's headroom and a hard wall when it blocks the receiving schools. The district will quote p.12 back at you: "By the report's own logic, we re-rate Bourbon Central and Cane Ridge, and the 59-seat problem vanishes." The report even anticipates this ("if the administration's answer is that ratings can be adjusted… that concedes the point," p.13) — but "concedes the point" is symmetric; it concedes the district's point too. What's left is preference, not arithmetic.
- **SEVERITY: HIGH.**
- **FIX:** Shift the receiving-school constraint from *rated capacity* to *physical/operational facts the pencil can't edit*: actual classroom counts, the Preschool Center already over capacity in both plans (p.12 table), Cane Ridge 453 in a building the 2013 plan rated 500 but now 422, section counts vs. rooms. Demand the room-by-room worksheets (already in Appendix B) and stop leaning on the 59-seat number until they arrive.

### 1.3 Secular enrollment decline the report's own chart proves
- **Where:** PDF p.16, Figure 11: 261 (1989) → 169 (2013) → 128 (2025). The report's counter is 46 hypothetical students: 30 rezoned + 16 HB 563 transfers (p.17).
- **Why exploitable:** Enrollment has halved over 36 years through every administration, and the report concedes the county is flat-to-shrinking, aging, and outside the growth corridor (p.15: "The district's enrollment decline is real and structural, and I will not pretend otherwise"). Against that, the growth plan rests on (a) rezoning Paris-area families *against their will* into a school 10 miles east — a political grenade the report never prices (those families vote too), and (b) 16 cross-county transfers with zero demand evidence — no survey, no waitlist, no letter of interest. The district: "Their rescue plan is a marketing hope and a forced busing of other people's children."
- **SEVERITY: HIGH.**
- **FIX:** Get real demand evidence before the vote: signed intent-to-enroll statements from actual families (in-zone returners, cross-county, preschool parents). Even 10 signatures converts a projection into a fact. Reframe rezoning as *opt-in first* ("open enrollment within district by proximity"), forced lines last.

### 1.4 "Their whole plan is a tax increase"
- **Where:** PDF p.21-22 and the site's "4 percent option" calculator: the levy is $386K year one, $1.2M by year three — the single largest line in Plan 3, without which the "menu" ($1.1-2.1M) barely clears closure's ceiling once soft lines (Medicaid, energy contracts, attendance campaigns, "shared services… deserve a serious, public study") are discounted.
- **Why exploitable:** A board facing a deficit can say: "The out-of-state author's alternative to a $600K recurring cut is raising your property taxes three years running, plus a menu of maybes." Several menu items are aspirational ranges with no implementation cost, no timeline, and admitted overlap ("ranges overlap and are not additive to the penny," p.21). The deficit demands *recurring, controllable* money; closure is the only line on the table entirely within the board's unilateral power that is also a pure cut.
- **SEVERITY: HIGH.**
- **FIX:** Re-order the menu cut-first (administrative rollback, attrition, transportation) and show a no-levy variant of Plan 3 that still beats Plan 2. Currently the report says the levy "is one option, not the only one" (p.22) but the arithmetic of Plan 3 quietly needs it.

### 1.5 Small-school score volatility — the report's caveat is the district's exhibit
- **Where:** PDF p.9: NMES 32.1 (2023) → 54.1 → 58.2; "the school's girls (85.7, the 91st percentile) far outpace its boys (28.8)." Site fact strip: "58.2 vs 26.5."
- **Why exploitable:** In 2023 — two years ago — NMES (32.1) scored *below* Cane Ridge (34.6, per the site's own chart data, index.html line 443). The girls/boys split shows the composite is a 128-student small-sample artifact: NMES boys (28.8) score at the level of the receiving schools. And the entire two-decade narrative runs on SchoolDigger's normalized score, which the report itself flags as "not KDE's official rating" (p.8, p.26) — the district will simply say "we use the state's official accountability system, not a website." The "best elementary in the region" headline is one bad 2026 test cycle away from collapse.
- **SEVERITY: MEDIUM-HIGH.**
- **FIX:** Lead with the three-year average (48.1 vs 26.4 vs 29.9 — already computed on p.9) and the official KDE indicators (proficiency rates, which are state-published: reading 50%, math 44%, writing +20, science +16, economically disadvantaged 57.1). Retire the boys/girls stat or convert it into a program ask; as printed it is pure ammunition.

### 1.6 The program-breadth argument — completely unaddressed
- **Where:** Nowhere. Search the PDF: there is no discussion of counselors, art/music/PE specials, interventionists, special-education service delivery, extracurriculars, teacher collaboration/PLCs, substitute coverage, or combined-grade risk at a 128-student school.
- **Why exploitable:** This is every superintendent's lead academic argument for consolidation: larger schools offer full-time specialists, richer programming, and resilient staffing that a 9-section school cannot. The report's silence lets the district own the "what's best for kids programmatically" frame unopposed — and it's the argument that moves board members who are uncomfortable with pure dollar talk.
- **SEVERITY: HIGH.**
- **FIX:** Add a section documenting what NMES students actually receive (gifted program is mentioned once, p.15) versus the receiving schools, and cite the small-schools literature the report already footnotes (Howley/Johnson/Petrie, source 16) on why program breadth doesn't translate to outcomes for low-income rural students.

### 1.7 Deferred maintenance flips into the district's favor
- **Where:** PDF p.13: $4.26M total need on a 1948/1963/1964 building rated for 174 — roughly $24,500 per seat, or $33,000 per *current* student.
- **Why exploitable:** The report answers that renovation money is restricted and can't fix the deficit (true), but restricted money is still scarce and rivalrous: every nickel-fund dollar into NMES is a dollar not into the $43.4M district-wide need or the CTE center. The report concedes closure "steers future SFCC offers of assistance… toward other projects" (p.11) — that *is* a real financial benefit of closure, understated as "a choice about priorities."
- **SEVERITY: MEDIUM.**
- **FIX:** Quantify the counter: the life-safety/accessibility slice is only $642,660 (p.12); the $3.62M renovation is a decade-old scope estimate awaiting the new condition assessment; and per-seat capital need at the crowded Paris schools (Preschool Center over capacity in two consecutive plans) should be shown side-by-side.

### 1.8 The runway argument cuts against "pause"
- **Where:** PDF p.5: "at the current pace of drawdown the unassigned reserve lasts roughly three more budget cycles"; p.26: "roughly three budget cycles in which to fix it properly."
- **Why exploitable:** Three cycles is not much. A closure enacted for 2027-28 needs a 2026 facility-plan decision; the report's ask — pause, answer ten questions, then run a two-year enrollment test with a fall 2028 checkpoint (p.26) — consumes most of the remaining runway before any structural fix lands. District: "Their plan is to study the problem until the reserves are gone."
- **SEVERITY: MEDIUM.**
- **FIX:** Attach dates. The ten questions are answerable in 60-90 days (open-records clocks are five business days); say so, and pair the pause with an explicit "board acts on the full menu at the September 2026 tax setting" schedule so the pause reads as sequencing, not delay.

## 2. RHETORICAL VULNERABILITIES — including breaches of the report's own no-motive pledge

The pledge (PDF p.28): *"this report criticizes decisions and asks for documents. It attributes no motive and alleges no wrongdoing to the superintendent, the finance office, the Board, or any member of the planning committee, and nothing in it should be read otherwise."* The district's counsel will read the following passages aloud next to it.

### 2.1 The "engineered decline" paragraph — the clearest breach
- **Quote (PDF p.16):** "Over the same years the district centralized preschool at the Paris center… the rated capacity was written down from 198 to 174; and no public recruitment or transfer program has marketed the county's highest-scoring elementary to anyone. **Each of those is a district decision, not a demographic fact**… **A school that stood 93 percent full against its rating in the 2021 plan did not become surplus in four years by itself.**"
- **Why exploitable:** "Did not become surplus by itself" is an unmistakable insinuation that the district manufactured the surplus. That is motive-attribution in everything but name. One quote destroys the "no motive" credential and re-brands the whole report as advocacy dressed as audit.
- **SEVERITY: CRITICAL.**
- **FIX:** Keep the factual sequence, delete the innuendo sentence, and replace with the neutral form already used elsewhere: "the records requested in Appendix B would show how much of the decline these decisions explain."

### 2.2 "The same pencil" — implying the capacity re-rating was gamed
- **Quote (PDF p.12-13):** "Rated capacity is a policy output the district shapes through its own room assignments… **the same pencil can raise North Middletown's rating back toward the 198 it carried in 2013**."
- **Why exploitable:** "The same pencil" insinuates the 198→174 write-down was instrumental rather than the routine, district-wide re-rating the report itself documents two sentences earlier ("The write-down was not unique: Cane Ridge fell from 500 to 422 and Bourbon Central from 564 to 521"). The report proves the innocent explanation, then implies the sinister one anyway.
- **SEVERITY: HIGH.**
- **FIX:** Drop "pencil"; state it as symmetry: "capacity ratings follow room assignments under 702 KAR 4:180, so they can be raised by the same lawful process that lowered them."

### 2.3 "Deferred investment itself becomes the closure case"
- **Quote (PDF p.13):** "when the same needs are priced in two consecutive plans, eight years apart, and the capital program builds elsewhere both times, **at what point does deferred investment itself become the closure case?**"
- **Why exploitable:** This is the classic "demolition by neglect" accusation — a strategy allegation. Rhetorical-question framing does not immunize it.
- **SEVERITY: HIGH.**
- **FIX:** Convert to the records ask it sits next to: "the maintenance and project records in Appendix B would show whether the 2013-priced work was completed."

### 2.4 Capital-program insinuation
- **Quote (PDF p.11):** "Not improper in itself, but it shows **a capital program being prioritized and cash-flowed in the same years the operating budget went into deficit.**" Plus Appendix B (p.30): "**Where $6.9 million of recent borrowing went**."
- **Why exploitable:** "Not improper in itself" is a lawyer's wink; "where the money went" implies the money is unaccounted for, when the report elsewhere states exactly where it went (Construction Fund, construction-in-progress, p.11). The district: "He knows where it went; he says so on the same page."
- **SEVERITY: MEDIUM.**
- **FIX:** Appendix B row: "The 2024 bond's stated project scope (official statement and BG-1)." Delete "not improper in itself."

### 2.5 "Run exactly this play" / "extracting it from one town"
- **Quotes:** PDF p.6: "Districts across the country have **run exactly this play**, and the results are on the record." PDF p.20 (staffing-alignment row): "spreads the adjustment fairly **instead of extracting it from one town**."
- **Why exploitable:** "Play" implies scheme; "extracting" implies targeting. Both characterize the board's motives, not its documents.
- **SEVERITY: MEDIUM.**
- **FIX:** "Districts across the country have tried this consolidation math, and the results are on the record." / "…rather than concentrating it on one school."

### 2.6 Website language is a full register harsher than the PDF
- **Quotes (index.html):** meta/OG line 11: "**The district's best elementary school is the one on the chopping block.**" Line 181: "**The school they want to close is the one that works.**" Line 140: "the town that loses its heartbeat."
- **Why exploitable:** "They want to close" attributes desire (who is "they"? — the board the campaign needs to persuade). "The one that works" states as fact that the other two schools *don't work* — an insult to the majority of the district's parents and teachers, resting on an unofficial third-party score. "Chopping block" is pure loaded language on the shareable OG card, which is what circulates on Facebook regardless of the PDF's care.
- **SEVERITY: HIGH** (the site is the mass-distribution surface; the pledge in its own footer — "attributes no motive and alleges no wrongdoing to anyone" — sits 200 lines below "the one they want to close").
- **FIX:** Hero/OG: "The district's highest-scoring elementary is the one proposed for closure. Here are the numbers." Retire "chopping block," "they want," "the one that works."

### 2.7 Calling out the superintendent's numbers by name
- **Quote (PDF p.3):** "Superintendent Larry Begley states the school serves about 100 students **(federal records show 128)**," repeated on the site (Q2: "public statements have ranged lower").
- **Why exploitable:** The parenthetical reads as "the superintendent misstates facts." Mild, but combined with 2.1-2.4 it forms a pattern the district will call a credibility campaign against a named individual.
- **SEVERITY: LOW-MEDIUM.**
- **FIX:** "Public statements and federal records differ (about 100 vs. 128); Question 2 asks which count the analysis uses."

### 2.8 The self-inflicted verification caveat
- **Quote (PDF p.1):** "**every figure should be re-verified against the cited primary sources before formal submission or republication**," plus the AI-assistance disclosure (p.1, p.28).
- **Why exploitable:** The district's cheapest possible response: "Even the author says his AI-assisted figures need re-verification. We'll rely on our auditors." It lets them dismiss the report without engaging a single number.
- **SEVERITY: MEDIUM.**
- **FIX:** Before submission to the board, do the re-verification, then change the line to "figures verified against the cited primary sources as of [date]; corrections policy on p.28." Keep the AI disclosure (honesty is an asset) but pair it with the human verification statement.

## 3. LEGAL / PROCEDURAL CLAIMS

### 3.1 KRS 157.370 — characterized essentially correctly, with two exposed edges
- **Quote (PDF p.18):** "KRS 157.370 sets transportation aid by transported pupils per square mile, paying more where density is low because low density costs more to serve." Cited with 702 KAR 3:270 (source 23, p.28).
- **Assessment:** Materially accurate. KRS 157.370 is the SEEK transportation adjustment; it computes aid from transported-pupil counts and a density scale (transported pupils per square mile of area served), with lower density earning a higher per-pupil allowance. Citing the companion regulation is correct practice. **Exposed edges:** (a) the density calculation is *district-wide*, so closing NMES doesn't change the funded density figure — the report actually uses this correctly (p.19-20), but should say "district-wide" explicitly to pre-empt a "he thinks aid is per-school" strawman; (b) the claim "the marginal state reimbursement on any NEW busing mile is zero" (p.20) is a consequence of the 2026-28 *appropriation freeze*, not of the statute — true today, but the district can note the 2024-26 budget restored funding to 100% and a future budget could again. Label it "under the current biennium's appropriation."
- **SEVERITY: LOW.** **FIX:** the two qualifiers above; the legal cite itself will survive scrutiny.

### 3.2 "$23.5" — identified: unused bonding capacity, and it's a double-edged exhibit
- **What it is:** PDF p.11 (twice) and Appendix B p.30: "The fiscal 2024 audit also notes the district held **$23.5 million in unused bonding capacity**." Also on the site (bonds card, line 173).
- **Assessment:** Used correctly (restricted, can't pay teachers — repeated each time). **But:** broadcasting a $23.5M headroom figure invites two district counters: (1) public confusion the district can exploit in reverse ("the report admits we have capacity for the career-technical center our students need — closure lets us pursue it without pouring $4.26M into a 128-student building"); (2) the figure is from the FY2024 audit, *before* the $6.055M 2024 issue consumed part of it — the report asks for the current KDE bonding potential statement (Appendix B), implicitly conceding its own number is stale.
- **SEVERITY: LOW-MEDIUM.** **FIX:** Always pair it as "$23.5M as of the FY2024 audit, before the 2024 issue; current figure requested," and pre-state the CTE-center counter: "the district can build its career center *and* keep NMES — the capacity math (Section 6) supports both."

### 3.3 Boston Public Schools — an easy target, and the site makes it worse
- **Quotes:** PDF p.20: "Boston Public Schools ran the signature version in 2017: an MIT-built routing algorithm produced bus routes 20 percent more efficient… saved roughly $5 million." Site (line 288): "**Boston proved the routing upside is real**… saved $5 million in one year."
- **Assessment:** The PDF is defensible — it uses Boston only as a *ceiling* and books just 5-10% of a $2.9M line ($145K-$290K), and it also cites in-state precedents (Fayette, Jefferson). But "comparing rural Kentucky to Boston" is exactly the one-liner the superintendent will use, and the website hands it to him by leading with Boston instead of Fayette/Jefferson. Boston: ~600+ buses, dense urban network, choice-driven busing, an MIT partnership — none of which maps to a district running an estimated three rural routes for NMES (p.19). Note the site's linked article title itself says "400 fewer buses" while the PDF says 50 — a discrepancy the district can nitpick (the study cut ~50 buses in year one; the 20%/400 figure is the algorithm's theoretical routing result).
- **SEVERITY: MEDIUM.** **FIX:** Lead with Fayette and Jefferson County (already cited, source 24) as the precedent; demote Boston to a footnote ("the upper bound in the literature"); reconcile the 50-vs-400 bus discrepancy or drop the number.

### 3.4 Open-records checklist — largely clean, with three exposures
- **Assessment:** Appendix B (p.30) is framed as document requests, explicitly disclaims seeking student-identifiable information, and each row names the estimate it would replace — good practice, hard to attack as harassment. Three exposures:
  1. **Snark residue:** "The building case, **if one exists**" and "Where $6.9 million of recent borrowing went" (see 2.4) — the only two rows with attitude; they let the district characterize fifteen neutral requests as an accusatory fishing expedition. FIX: neutral phrasing.
  2. **Requester standing:** the checklist says Kentucky's ORA "entitles **any resident** to these documents" (p.30) — correct as a statement of law (post-2021, KRS 61.870(10) limits requests to Kentucky residents), but the author states "Bourbon County is no longer my home, or my family's" (p.23). If he is no longer a Kentucky resident, *he cannot file these requests himself*; the district can lawfully deny him and score a procedural win. FIX: have a named local resident (parent, the mayor, the pastor quoted on p.22) file the requests; say so publicly.
  3. **Volume optics:** fifteen requests with a five-business-day clock invites "burying the office in paperwork three weeks before the school year." Note KRS 61.872 also permits time extensions where records are in active use/storage — don't promise the public a five-day turnaround the statute doesn't guarantee. FIX: prioritize five requests (net-savings worksheet, condition assessment, bond OS/BG-1, T-1 report, capacity worksheets) and mark the rest as follow-ups.
- **SEVERITY: MEDIUM** (item 2 is the sharpest).

### 3.5 Process description (702 KAR 4:180, waiver claim)
- **Assessment:** The multi-step process (committee → KDE review → committee vote → board adoption → public hearing → KBE approval) and "transitional ≠ closure" are stated accurately (p.3, glossary p.29) — this discipline is a strength. The claim that KDE "has granted other districts" waivers/extensions of the four-year planning deadline (p.25) is asserted without a cited instance. **FIX:** cite one example or soften to "the regulation permits a waiver request."

## 4. AUDIENCE RISK — what alienates the board, and hostages to fortune

### 4.1 The out-of-town messenger (the district's best ad hominem)
- **Quote (PDF p.23):** "**Bourbon County is no longer my home, or my family's.**" Site: "Written by Dr. Ryan Bradley, a former NMES King."
- **Why exploitable:** The campaign's core financial ask is that *local* taxpayers accept a three-year levy escalation — urged by someone who concedes he won't pay a cent of it and cannot even file the open-records requests (see 3.4.2). "An out-of-state alumnus and an AI want to raise your taxes" is the district's entire closing argument, gift-wrapped.
- **SEVERITY: CRITICAL.**
- **FIX:** Do not hide the authorship (honesty is established); *add local principals*: co-sign the report with resident parents, the mayor, Rev. Dr. Moon; route the records requests and the board ask through them. Reframe the personal note as "why I did the work; the ask belongs to the people who live here."

### 4.2 Trashing the receiving schools insults most of the electorate
- **Quotes:** PDF p.9: consolidation "would move children from the district's strongest elementary environment **into its weakest ones**"; site line 182: receiving schools "sit in the **bottom quarter of the state**"; Q9 (p.24): "schools scoring 26.5 and 19.3."
- **Why exploitable:** Bourbon Central and Cane Ridge enroll 912 children — seven times NMES — and their parents, teachers, and the board members who represent them are the persuasion targets. Every "your school is failing" framing converts a potential ally into an opponent and lets the board recast the fight as NMES vs. everyone else. The rebalancing plan simultaneously asks those same schools to *give up* 30 students.
- **SEVERITY: HIGH.**
- **FIX:** Reframe every comparison as districtwide benefit: "rebalancing relieves crowded Paris classrooms and preserves what's working in the east — every school gains." Move raw score comparisons out of headlines into the data section; the board can be shown the gap privately in Q9 without it being the website's second H2.

### 4.3 Factual hostages to fortune
- "**58.2 vs 26.5**" (site fact strip) — unofficial SchoolDigger metric presented without caveat on the hero; one new score release or a "that's not the state's rating" from KDE undercuts the headline. (Caveat exists at p.8/p.26 and in the chart footnote — but not where the number is biggest.) **SEVERITY: MEDIUM. FIX:** footnote the strip or switch to official proficiency figures.
- "**$250K to $600K — the realistic range**" (site) — the author's own estimate presented with the same certainty he criticizes the district's "$1M" for. If the district publishes a worksheet showing $800K, the campaign's credibility, not the district's, takes the hit. **FIX:** "our estimate from the public record — the district can replace it with a worksheet any day (we've asked)."
- **Girls 85.7 / boys 28.8** (p.9) — see 1.5; as printed it's the district's best single stat.
- **2015-16 boundary vintage** (p.18, flagged in a footnote) — if boundaries changed since, the 110-square-mile/38% map and the density table collapse; the site's map card doesn't carry the caveat. **FIX:** put the vintage caveat on the map itself.
- **FRPL "roughly 76 to 93 percent"** (p.26) vs "about three-quarters" (p.7) and "roughly three-quarters or more" — a 17-point source spread the report uses at whichever end suits the sentence. **FIX:** pick the conservative bound consistently.
- **The fall-2028 target of 145 students** (p.26) — commendably falsifiable, but it hands the district a tripwire: "even the advocates concede that if they can't find 17 kids, 'the conversation changes.'" Keep it, but only alongside the district's reciprocal commitment.
- **The mass-email button and petition** (index.html lines 353-359) publishing all six officials' addresses with a pre-written subject line — effective mobilization, but boards under form-email bombardment harden; and pairing "we attribute no wrongdoing" with a pressure-campaign toolkit lets the district call the neutrality a pose. **FIX:** rewrite the template to ask a question rather than demand ("Will the board publish the net-savings worksheet before voting?") and encourage personalization.

## 5. THE THREE CHANGES WITH THE HIGHEST PERSUASION ROI
(what I, as the district's counsel, would least want fixed)

### #1 — Purge the insinuation thread and resolve the capacity contradiction (items 2.1-2.4, 1.2)
Right now I can win without touching the finances: I read the "attributes no motive" pledge (p.28), then read "did not become surplus in four years by itself" (p.16), "the same pencil" (p.12), "deferred investment itself become the closure case" (p.13), and "the one they want to close" (site) — credibility gone in ninety seconds. Then I use the report's own "capacity is a policy output" concession to erase the 59-seat argument. If the author scrubs six sentences and adopts one consistent capacity framing (physical rooms, not ratings), I lose both my character attack and my capacity rebuttal, and the remaining document is genuinely hard to discredit — it concedes the deficit, praises the auditors, and cites primary sources.

### #2 — Pre-empt the full-absorption savings model (item 1.1)
My path to validating "over a million dollars" runs straight through the report's own numbers: 9 sections averaging 14 kids, caps of 24, 9 × $85K + $290K fixed ≈ $1.05M. The report's $640K "ceiling" and the calculator's 6-position slider cap are the two things I will demolish first, and when the campaign's headline range breaks, everything downstream (the fact strip, the plans table, the $400-500K board threshold) breaks with it. If the report instead publishes the grade-by-grade absorption scenario itself — showing where added sections become unavoidable and netting busing, transition, and family-attrition costs against the 9-position gross — the district's worksheet arrives pre-rebutted, and the burden of proof stays where the report put it.

### #3 — Localize the messenger, soften the school-vs-school framing, and lead the alternatives with cuts (items 4.1, 4.2, 1.4, 3.4.2)
My closing argument to the community is one sentence: "A man who no longer lives here, working with an AI, wants to raise your taxes and calls your children's schools the district's weakest." Every clause of that sentence is currently quotable. Local co-signers filing the records requests (also curing the ORA-residency defect), a "every school in the county gains" reframe, and an alternatives menu re-ordered so administrative rollback and transportation savings precede the levy would take the sentence away from me — and it costs the campaign nothing analytically, because the underlying numbers don't change.

---

**Bottom line:** The report's financial architecture (two pots of money, transfers-vs-operating presentation, closure-savings literature, the ten-questions device) is strong and the sourcing discipline is genuine. The exposure is concentrated in (a) one over-optimistic staffing assumption that props up the headline savings range, (b) a half-dozen sentences of insinuation that breach the report's own neutrality pledge, and (c) a messenger/tone problem the website amplifies. All three are cheaply fixable — which is exactly why, as opposing counsel, I'd rather the project never reads this review.
