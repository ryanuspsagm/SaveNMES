# Independent Review of the SaveNMES Claims

**Branch:** `austin-cursor` · **Date:** July 20, 2026  
**What this is:** A second, plain-language review of the big claims on the Save North Middletown Elementary website and financial model. It is not a recommendation to close or keep the school. It asks: *Do the numbers and arguments hold up?*

---

## Bottom line

Many of the audit and facility-plan figures on the site are copied correctly. The overall story is still **mixed to fragile**. The site is right that the district has a real money problem and that closing one 128-student school is unlikely to fix it alone. Several of its sharpest comparisons, though, overstate how solid the “closure loses / other options win” case is.

**Trust the site for:** the size of the budget hole, the wall between building money and teacher pay, and the need for the district to show its own worksheets.  
**Trust it less for:** how many open seats the receiving schools have today, how big the “alternatives” package really is, and how far kids would ride if North Middletown closed.

---

## What checks out

- The district’s main operating fund ran about a **$2.65 million** shortfall last year (before transfers), after about **$2.54 million** the year before. Cash reserves are falling by roughly **$1.1 million** a year. Those figures match the public audits the site cites.
- Under Kentucky rules, **building and bond money cannot pay teachers**. Closing a school does not automatically create cash for salaries.
- In the state’s 2021 facility plan, North Middletown was listed at **174** seats with **161** enrolled—about **93%** full. The earlier 2013 plan listed it at **198**. Both plans called the school permanent.
- Cane Ridge’s seats-on-paper figure of **422**, and North Middletown renovation costs of about **$1.9 million** (2013) and **$4.3 million** (2021), match the facility plans.
- Bourbon’s property-tax rate (**52.4 cents** per $100) is second-lowest among nine nearby districts. A multi-year 4% revenue path can cover a large share of the headline shortfall by year three—not all in year one.
- Even the site’s own closure range (**$250,000 to $600,000** a year) does not come close to filling a multi-million-dollar hole.

---

## What is shaky or overstated

**1. Two different “holes” get mixed together (biggest problem)**  
The site often talks about a **$2.65 million** shortfall. When it says a recovery plan “restores balance,” it is leaning on the smaller **~$1.1 million** yearly drop in reserves. A plan can look like it “fixes” the smaller number while still leaving the bigger one open. That makes alternatives look stronger—and closure look weaker—than a single yardstick would show.

**2. Open seats at Bourbon Central may be overstated**  
The site says Bourbon Central has **549** seats today, leaving a net of **59** open seats across the two receiving schools for **128** North Middletown children. But the 2021 facility plan’s *current* line for Bourbon Central is **521** seats. The **549** figure is a “to become” number after an addition. If you use **521**, net open seats fall to about **31**, and the shortfall jumps toward roughly **97** children. That matters: tighter space means more staff, classrooms, or portables—costs that are not fully baked into the “closure savings” story. (Even at 59 seats, 128 kids do not fit without crowding somewhere.)

**3. The $1.1–$2.1 million “alternatives” total is partly guesswork**  
That combined band is not a simple add-up of the menu rows. The authors trimmed overlapping ideas and typed in a “conservative” range. Some ideas may double-count (for example, talking about a tax increase both inside the menu and again as “menu plus levy”). Year-one tax dollars also do not quite match between the website and the spreadsheet. Parents can walk away thinking there is a ready **$1 million-plus** no-closure fix when parts of that total are still optimistic or unfinished plans.

**Other softer spots (shorter):**  
“About four extra road miles” is mostly map distance stretched by a factor, not the district’s actual bus routes. Test-score gaps are real in the charts the site uses, but school averages are not proof every moved child will do worse. “Grow into a premier elementary” is a vision, not a funded program—and enrollment has been falling for years.

---

## What still looks fair to say

The money problem is real and districtwide. Closing North Middletown is unlikely to erase it. Building dollars are not teacher dollars. The school was nearly full against its official rating in the last approved plan. Before any vote, the board should publish real numbers for receiving-school space grade by grade, one-time transition costs, and busing—not only rely on an advocacy model. The site is useful when it asks for those documents.

---

## Recommendation

Use the site as a **source-linked briefing**, not as the final scorecard. Before leaning on its headline comparisons, three things should be clear in writing:

1. **One definition** of the budget hole for every “percent covered” and “restores balance” claim.  
2. **Bourbon Central seats shown both ways**—521 today vs 549 if/when an addition is finished—with open-seat math recalculated.  
3. **Alternatives totals rebuilt** from non-overlapping lines, or clearly labeled as the authors’ judgment.

**Safest takeaway for a board or parent:** Closure savings are probably modest next to the full problem. The advocacy site’s strongest “closure loses / menu wins” framing is not yet solid enough to carry a decision by itself.
